## Problem Statement

### Setup

Fix dimension $s\ge 1$ . For each $m\in\mathbb N$ , consider random linear scrambling of a digital net generated by matrices $C_1,\dots,C_s\in\{0,1\}^{E\times m}$ . In the Walsh-analysis of the source paper, let $Z(k)\in\{0,1\}$ denote the indicator that the frequency $k\in\mathbb N_*^s$ survives the scrambling. For each $r\in\mathbb N$ , let

$
\mathcal V_r:=\{V\subset \mathbb N_*^s:\ |V|=\operatorname{rank}(V)=r\},
$

and define the $r$ -way rank deficiency

$
R_{m,r}:=mr+\sup_{V\in\mathcal V_r}\log_2\mathbb P\bigl(Z(k)=1\text{ for all }k\in V\bigr).
$

The source proves existence of generating matrices with

$
R_{m,r}\le (2r+2^r-1)s\log_2(m+1)\qquad\text{for all }r\ge 1,
$

which is enough for asymptotic coverage of the paper's quantile intervals.

### Unsolved Problem

Exhibit, characterize, or prove existence of generating matrices for which the sharper uniform bound

$
R_{m,r}\le C r\log_2(m+1)
$

holds for all $m,r$ (with $C>0$ depending at most on $s$ ). As noted in Remark 8 of the source, such a bound would upgrade the interval theory to the stronger $m^{-1/2}$ convergence rate from Theorem 7.

## Significance & Implications

This problem isolates the combinatorial obstruction behind the current QMC interval rate. A linear-in- $r$ rank-deficiency bound would sharpen the source paper's asymptotic-coverage result to the more desirable $m^{-1/2}$ regime and would substantially improve structural understanding of which digital-net generating matrices are optimal for uncertainty quantification.

## Known Partial Results

- The source paper notes that common Sobol and Niederreiter-type generating matrices are asymptotically full-rank at the one-way level, i.e. $R_{m,1}$ stays bounded.

- It proves that when the generating matrices are drawn uniformly from $I_m$ , with high probability one has $R_{m,r}\le (2r+2^r-1)s\log_2(m+1)$ for all $r$ , and hence deterministic matrices with this weaker all- $r$ bound exist.

- The source further shows that a stronger linear-in- $r$ bound would imply the sharper $m^{-1/2}$ convergence rate, but no such construction is given there.
